Data Visualization¶

Old way¶

  • Named plots:

    • histogram, scatterplot, time series plot, etc.

  • Increasingly complicated named plots (e.g. Hexbin plot with marginal distributions)

  • Lacks versitility: many options as you build a plot, a named plot can only give you so many options

New way¶

  • Deconstructing the plot: understand and name the components
  • Grammar of graphics: rules that guide how these fit together

Graphical excellence¶

  • shows the data
  • induce the viewer to think about substance rather than methodology
  • avoid distortion of data
  • present many numbers in small space
  • make large datasets coherent
  • encourage the eye to compare different pieces of data
  • reveal the data at several levels of detail
  • serve a reasonably clear purpose: description, exploration, tabulation, or decoration
  • be closely integrated with the statistical and verbal descriptions of a data set

(The Visual Display of Quantitative Info., Tufte)

  • Caption translated to English: "The numbers of men present are represented by the widths of the colored zones at a rate of one millimeter for every ten thousand men; they are further written across the zones. The [tan] designates the men who enter Russia, the black those who leave it."
  • Original image of Minard's graphic from John Schneider's Napoleonic Literature page, linked on http://www.datavis.ca/gallery/re-minard.php

  • Powerpoint reproduction from http://extremepresentation.typepad.com/blog/2010/06/

Deconstructing this plot¶

Elements of the plot¶

  • geometric objects (geoms): points
  • coordinate systems: linear scale and Euclidean coordinates
  • annotation for the plot: labels, legend

Aesthetic mappings¶

  • aesthetic elements: X-position, Y-position, color
  • aesthetic mappings: function mapping variable to aesthetic element

Example aesthetic mapping¶

Linear map from Series, x, to [0,1]. $$ f(x) = \frac{x - x.min()}{x.max() - x.min()} $$ Mapping is appropriate for position.

Scale¶

A scale is a function applied to the variable before/during the aethetic mapping, $$ g(x) = \log(x), $$ so that final map is $f(g(x))$.

Layered grammar of graphics¶

In addition to basic elements:

  • geoms
  • aesthetics
  • aesthetic mappings

Also have:

  • scales, coordinate systems
  • layers
  • stats
  • facetting

Layers¶

  • contains geoms, statistical transformation, and possibly a position adjustment
  • can inherit the dataset and aesthetic mappings from default layer
  • overlayed ontop of one another

Coordinate Systems¶

  • Euclidean coords uses X,Y aesthetics to determine positions
  • Radial coords uses radius and angle to determine positions
  • Scale can be applied independently of coord

Stats (statistical transformations)¶

  • take the data as input and outputs new data
  • transformation can be dependent on aspects of the current plot (such as scales)
  • scale modifies the aesthetic mapping, stats modify a layer (or creates its own layer)

Facetting¶

  • group by a variable or equation
  • creates plots for each subset
  • by default stats are applied groupwise
